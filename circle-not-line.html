Why the circle is not a line.

A GitHub-exclusive and "eternally" ongoing archive of proofs explaining why the circle \(S^1\) is not homeomorphic to the real line \(\mathbb{R}\).

The project began from a very ordinary mathematical question asked under very non-ordinary pressure: a room, a definition, a silence, and the sudden feeling that topology itself had filed paperwork against me.

About this page

This is not a course project. It is a personal mathematical archive: a collection of different proofs, invariants, obstructions, and viewpoints around one deceptively simple question.

The origin story

Kafkaesque topology

At some point, I was asked why the circle is not a line. Under calm circumstances, this is a friendly question. Under interview lighting, it becomes a small bureaucratic nightmare: the definition of homeomorphism waits at the desk, the inverse map asks for missing documentation, and the mind discovers that it has arrived without the correct form.

The purpose of this page is not revenge against a question. It is more dignified than that, but only slightly. It is a way of turning a failed moment into a growing archive: whenever I find another way to see why \(S^1\) is not \(\mathbb{R}\), I add it here.

Further answers

If you know another way to explain why the circle is not a line — preferably one not yet filed in this small topological bureaucracy — feel free to contact me at constgogotsis@gmail.com. I would be glad to add further proofs, viewpoints, or obstructions to this archive.

The file remains open, not because the answer is uncertain, but because the applicant keeps returning with new paperwork. Each proof below is another stamped refusal in the line’s attempt to acquire circular citizenship.

Proof archive

Each entry records a different defect in the application. The proposed homeomorphism is presented at the counter; the circle clerk inspects one document at a time. In one file, compactness is missing. In another, removing a point makes the papers split into two disconnected pieces. Elsewhere, some invariant refuses to sign. The conclusion is always the same: application denied.

1

Compactness obstruction

Global topology

Approach: compare a topological property preserved by homeomorphisms.

The circle \(S^1\) is compact as a closed and bounded subset of \(\mathbb{R}^2\). The real line \(\mathbb{R}\) is not compact.

\[ S^1=\{(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2:x^2+y^2=1\}. \]

If there were a homeomorphism \(f:S^1\to\mathbb{R}\), then \(\mathbb{R}\) would be the continuous image of a compact space, hence compact. This is false. Therefore \(S^1\) is not homeomorphic to \(\mathbb{R}\).

2

Removing one point

Connectedness

Approach: delete one point and compare what remains.

If \(S^1\) and \(\mathbb{R}\) were homeomorphic, then deleting a point from each corresponding space would produce homeomorphic remainders.

But for any \(p\in S^1\), the space \(S^1\setminus\{p\}\) is connected; in fact it is homeomorphic to an open interval. On the other hand, for any \(q\in\mathbb{R}\), the space \(\mathbb{R}\setminus\{q\}\) has two connected components:

\[ \mathbb{R}\setminus\{q\}=(-\infty,q)\cup(q,\infty). \]

Connectedness is preserved by homeomorphism. Therefore the two punctured spaces cannot be homeomorphic, and hence \(S^1\not\cong\mathbb{R}\).
