What this page is

A mathematical inventory, not a single endpoint.

This page collects the raw material of a developing direction. Its purpose is to keep together the convex-geometric language of Newton polytopes, the algebraic language of sparse polynomial systems, the algorithmic language of mixed volume computation, and the computational experiments that make the subject concrete.

Reservoir

Organized accumulation

The page records concepts, papers, examples, code fragments, and future project branches before they become polished standalone articles.

Bridge

Planned explainer

Supports and Newton polytopes

The support of a polynomial records which monomials appear. Its convex hull is the Newton polytope, turning algebraic sparsity into convex geometry.

sparse polynomials convex hulls
Planned explainer

Minkowski sums

The operation \(A+B=\{a+b:a\in A,\ b\in B\}\) is the natural addition of convex bodies and prepares the ground for mixed volume.

convex geometry polytope arithmetic
Core concept

Mixed volume

Mixed volume is the coefficient hidden inside the volume polynomial of a Minkowski combination. It measures how several polytopes interact.

volume polynomial BKK bound
Theorem shelf

Bernstein's theorem

The number of isolated solutions of a generic sparse system in the algebraic torus is controlled by the mixed volume of the Newton polytopes.

torus solutions generic systems
Algebraic bridge

Sparse resultants

Resultants encode elimination. In the sparse setting, their structure is governed by supports, Newton polytopes, and mixed-volume phenomena.

Status

A reservoir under construction.

This page is meant to change as the direction becomes clearer. The current stage is collection and orientation: mapping the objects, deciding which papers deserve full treatment, and building small computational experiments.

Phase I

Collect the objects

Stabilize the core vocabulary: supports, Newton polytopes, Minkowski sums, mixed volume, sparse systems, resultants, and separation bounds.

Phase II

Build the paper shelf

Add exact links, reading notes, and short descriptions of the papers that define the direction. Decide which papers deserve full project pages.

Phase III

Open the Julia laboratory

Start from minimal examples and slowly build toward exact mixed volume computations, with every experiment tied to a mathematical explanation.

Phase IV

Branch into finished pages

Turn selected parts of the inventory into polished pages: explainers, undergraduate translations, computational notes, and research-oriented project pages.

The reservoir should remain useful even before it is complete: a visible map of the material already collected, the material being understood, and the material waiting to become a separate project.
