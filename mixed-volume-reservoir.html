Highlighted paper

Sparse resultants and mixed volume computation.

This paper is one of the technical starting points for the reservoir. It studies sparse polynomial systems through their monomial supports and Newton polytopes, and uses that structure to build algorithms for sparse resultants and mixed volume.

Canny–Emiris

Efficient Incremental Algorithms for the Sparse Resultant and the Mixed Volume

The point of the paper is not only to define mixed volume, but to make it computational. Instead of treating a polynomial system only through its total degrees, the paper uses the monomials that actually occur. Those supports determine Newton polytopes, and the geometry of these polytopes guides the construction of smaller sparse resultant matrices.

This is why the paper belongs here: it sits exactly at the intersection I want to understand better — sparse algebra, convex geometry, elimination, and implementable algorithms. A full explainer page will later unpack the paper step by step.

Newton polytopes

A polynomial support becomes a finite set of exponent vectors. Its convex hull is the Newton polytope, turning algebraic sparsity into geometry.

Mixed volume

Mixed volume is the multilinear coefficient hidden in the volume of a Minkowski combination. It measures how several polytopes interact.

Minkowski sums BKK bound

Bernstein’s theorem

For a generic sparse system, the number of isolated solutions in the algebraic torus is controlled by the mixed volume of the Newton polytopes.
