The DMM Bound.

An integrated project page based on a Computational Algebra presentation on the Davenport–Mahler–Mignotte philosophy: from separation bounds for one polynomial to separation information for sparse polynomial systems.

The guiding idea is simple: reduce a multivariate system to a univariate object by projection and elimination, apply the known one-variable bound, and then transfer the information back to distances between solutions.

Project overview

The presentation does not try to reproduce the full technical paper. Its purpose is to expose the central mechanism: how the univariate DMM bound becomes useful for multivariate polynomial systems.

Reduction

Why separation bounds?

In numerical or symbolic solving, approximating roots is not enough. We need enough information to verify and isolate them. A separation bound tells us how close two distinct roots can be.

The computational problem

Isolation

Suppose we want to solve a polynomial system

\[ f_1(x)=\cdots=f_n(x)=0. \]

Even when numerical approximations are available, algorithms still need to decide whether two approximations correspond to the same solution or to two different solutions.

Aggregate bounds

Product

A usual separation bound controls the closest pair:

\[ \operatorname{sep}(f)=\min_{i\ne j}|\gamma_i-\gamma_j|. \]

The DMM philosophy is more global: it controls from below products of selected distances.

\[ \prod_{(i,j)\in\Omega}|\gamma_i-\gamma_j|. \]

The one-variable model

The univariate case is the model. A polynomial has roots already living on a one-dimensional object, so the DMM bound can speak directly about products of distances between those roots.

A polynomial and its roots

DMM₁

Let

\[ f(X)=a_d\prod_{i=1}^{d}(X-\gamma_i)\in\mathbb{C}[X]. \]

The Davenport–Mahler–Mignotte theorem gives lower bounds for products of distances between selected roots. In the integer-coefficient case, the bound can be expressed through the degree and coefficient size of \(f\), equivalently through quantities such as the Mahler measure.

The key point is that DMM is not merely about the single closest pair of roots. It gives a more collective estimate on how roots can cluster.

From systems to one variable

For polynomial systems, roots do not live on a line. The proof therefore constructs a one-variable object by projecting the solutions and eliminating the original variables.

The basic sparse system

Laurent polynomials

The central setting is a system of \(n\) Laurent polynomials in \(n\) variables:

\[ (\Sigma):\quad f_1(x)=\cdots=f_n(x)=0, \qquad x=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\in(\mathbb{C}^{*})^n. \]

Each polynomial has finite support:

\[ f_i=\sum_j c_{i,j}x^{a_{i,j}}. \]

Bernstein bound

Mixed volume

If \(Q_i\) is the Newton polytope of \(f_i\), the mixed volume

\[ M_0=\operatorname{MV}(Q_1,\ldots,Q_n) \]

bounds the number \(D\) of isolated toric solutions:

\[ D\le M_0. \]

Why not only Bézout?

Sparse geometry

Bézout uses only degrees and gives a bound of the form \(d^n\). The Bernstein bound uses which monomials actually occur. For sparse systems, the mixed-volume bound may be substantially sharper.

Projection and the \(u\)-resultant

The reduction chooses a linear form that separates the solutions. Then the system is eliminated into a single polynomial in the new parameter \(u\).

Projecting the solutions

Linear form

Introduce a linear equation with a new parameter:

\[ f_0=u+r_1x_1+\cdots+r_nx_n. \]

On a solution \(\gamma_j\), this gives

\[ u_j=-(r_1\gamma_{j,1}+\cdots+r_n\gamma_{j,n}). \]

Separating element

Distinct images

We want a linear form that does not identify two distinct solutions:

\[ \gamma_i\ne\gamma_j\quad\Longrightarrow\quad u_i\ne u_j. \]

The presentation uses a finite family of linear forms

\[ \ell_i(x)=x_1+ix_2+\cdots+i^{\,n-1}x_n. \]

At least one of them separates the \(D\) solutions.

The \(u\)-resultant

Elimination

Once \(f_0\) is chosen, eliminate \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\):

\[ f_0=f_1=\cdots=f_n=0 \quad\rightsquigarrow\quad U(u)=0. \]

The roots of \(U\) are precisely the projected values \(u_j\) associated to the solutions of the original system, assuming the projection is separating.

The multivariate bound

The proof of the multivariate statement is not a completely new theorem from zero. It is the one-variable theorem after projection and elimination, followed by a comparison of distances.

Proof structure

One line

Reduce the system to \(U(u)\), apply the known univariate bound, then compare \(|u_i-u_j|\) with \(\|\gamma_i-\gamma_j\|\).

Comparing distances

Cauchy–Schwarz

If

\[ u_i-u_j=-\sum_{k=1}^{n}r_k(\gamma_{i,k}-\gamma_{j,k}), \]

then Cauchy–Schwarz gives

\[ |u_i-u_j| \le \left(\sum_{k=1}^{n}r_k^2\right)^{1/2} \|\gamma_i-\gamma_j\|. \]

Hence

\[ \|\gamma_i-\gamma_j\| \ge \left(\sum_{k=1}^{n}r_k^2\right)^{-1/2} |u_i-u_j|. \]

The basic DMMₙ statement

Product bound

For isolated solutions \(\gamma_1,\ldots,\gamma_D\in(\mathbb{C}^{*})^n\), the multivariate DMM theorem gives lower bounds for products of the form

\[ \prod_{(i,j)\in\Omega}\|\gamma_i-\gamma_j\|. \]

In the integer-coefficient case, it also gives information about coordinate bounds and the separation number \(\operatorname{sep}(\Sigma)\).

What changes from DMM₁?

Pattern

DMM₁ has one polynomial and roots already on a line. DMMₙ has a system, a separating projection, a \(u\)-resultant, and mixed volume instead of the degree-only Bézout count.

What the bound depends on

Parameters

The resulting estimates depend on the number of solutions, coefficient size, the separating form, and the mixed volumes of the Newton polytopes.

Applications and extensions

The presentation closes by indicating why such bounds matter: they control precision, subdivision complexity, and the size of computations in concrete algebraic problems.

Positive-dimensional systems

Extension

If the solution set contains curves or surfaces, the simple resultant picture is no longer enough. The same philosophy continues, but the method needs a more technical adaptation to isolate the relevant zero-dimensional information.

Eigenvectors and eigenvalues

Application

For an integer matrix \(A\), eigenvalue–eigenvector pairs can be encoded as a polynomial system:

\[ Av=\lambda v, \qquad \sum_i v_i^2=1. \]

Mixed volume can give a smaller bound than a naive Bézout estimate, which leads to better control on the number of bits needed in the computation.

Subdivision algorithms

Root isolation

Subdivision methods isolate roots by repeatedly partitioning the search region. If two roots are very close, more subdivisions may be needed. DMM-type bounds control the total number of subdivision steps required.

Main takeaway

The presentation’s core message is that multivariate separation information can be approached by a disciplined reduction to one variable.

The mechanism in one line

Conclusion

If the solutions of a system can be brought onto one variable without collisions, then the known one-variable separation bound becomes a tool for controlling distances in the original multivariate system.

What is useful

Separation bounds are not only theoretical estimates. They tell us how much precision is needed to distinguish solutions in practice.

What is hidden

The precise constants and technical estimates of the resultant are left outside this exposition. The page keeps the structural mechanism.

What remains

The positive-dimensional case shows that the same method has a deeper continuation beyond the basic isolated-solution setting.
